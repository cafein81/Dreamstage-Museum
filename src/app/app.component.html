Dreamstage Museum

Welcome to the Dreamstage Brain and Sleep Science Museum

Over a long and successful career as a sleep and dream researcher, Dr. J. Allan Hobson collected numerous unique pictures, books and other items related to his field of study. His museum offers private access to this collection. Since the museum is located in a remote area of Vermont's "Northeast Kingdom," its exhibits have previously only been accessible to local groups and intrepid travelers. Now you can visit it virtually. So feel free to come in and press the buttons. See Dr. Hobson's private collection and listen to his personal commentaries for a guided tour. Learn about the studies and interests of one of the world's leading experts on sleep, dreams and consciousness.

Dr. J. Allan Hobson

Allan Hobson received his A.B. from Wesleyan University (with honors) in 1955, and his M.D. from Harvard University in 1959. He was introduced to sleep and dream research by Frederick Snyder and Edward Evarts at the National Institute of Mental Health in Bethesda, Maryland, and studied with Michel Jouvet in Lyon, France.

Dr. Hobson was a Professor of Psychiatry at the Harvard Medical School and directed the Laboratory of Neurophysiology at the Massachusetts Mental Health Center in Boston from 1968 to 2003. He has published more than 200 peer-reviewed articles and 10 books on sleep, dreaming and consciousness. His theoretical contributions include the reciprocal interaction model of sleep cycle control and the activation-synthesis theory of dreaming (with Robert McCarley), the AIM model of conscious state variation, and the protoconsciousness theory of dream function. He lectures throughout the world on consciousness, sleep and dreaming.

He is a member of the American Association for the Advancement of Science, the Association for the Psychophysiological Study of Sleep, the National Institute of Mental Health, the Sleep Research Society, the Society for Neuroscience, and the U.S. Public Health Service. He is the recipient of the Distinguished Investigator Award of the Sleep Research Society, the Farrell Prize of the division of Sleep Medicine at Harvard Medical School, the von Humboldt award of the Max Planck Society, and the Benjamin Rush Gold Medal for the best scientific exhibit (for Dreamstage), from the American Psychiatric Association.

The North Star Farm in East Burke, Vermont

Dr. Hobson bought this property for a second home in 1965. By employing local carpenters and other workers, he has spent many years happily restoring the buildings, and continues to make improvements to this day.

The North Star Farm Barn

This barn was once the heart of a working dairy farm, originally started by an Irish immigrant in 1860. Now it has been restored and houses the Dreamstage Museum. The second floor is partitioned into the entry, the Dreamstage Exhibit, the office, the gallery and a storage area. The silo is now Dr. Hobson's library.

The Dreamstage Exhibition first opened at Harvard University in April of 1977. It toured around the U.S. from 1978 to 1980 and was recreated in Bordeaux, France in 1982. After the exhibit stopped touring, Dr. Hobson stored it in his barn. It now forms the centerpiece of his Dreamstage Museum.

The artworks and photographs displayed include works by Rafael Lorente de No, Santiago Ramon y Cajal, Facundo Valverde, Thomas Woolsey, Theodore Spagna, Arnold Scheibel, Alvin Berman, Clinton Woolsey, David Scott, Ruth Bleier and John Woolsey.

Dreamstage Poster from the original tour.

Santiago Ramon y Cajal drawing of a frog's spinal cord.

The Spanish neuroanatomist Santiago Ramon y Cajal first proposed that the brain cell (or neuron) was the structural unit of the nervous system. Our brains contain roughly 100 billion neurons which work together to produce our waking and dreaming states of consciousness.

Santiago Ramon y Cajal drawing of nerve cells.

Working with Cajal, Lorente de No studied the regeneration of nerve cells as may occur when we have accidents or diseases of the brain. How much recovery the brain can achieve is still unknown. Many of the neurons drawn by Lorente were in the cerebral cortex and brainstem.

Facundo Valverde drawing of nervous system connections.

Each neuron contacts at least 10,000 others via its axons and receives information from thousands of other brain cells. The contact points, called synapses, can be on the large body of the cell or on its spider-like processes, the dendrites, which have tiny spines that are specialized receivers of brain information.

Thomas Woolsey computer-generated image of a neuron and a photograph of nerve cells.

In order to facilitate the study of the detailed structure of neurons, Thomas Woolsey designed a computerized system for representing cell bodies, axons, and dendrites. Thomas is the scientist son of Clinton Woolsey.

Theodore Spagna time-lapse photographs of a sleeper and his cat, showing their movements throughout their sleep cycles.

Using a Zeiss Ikon time-lapse camera, Spagna recorded the sleep of his friend Peter and Peter's cat. Peter's frequent posture shifts were indicative of his poor sleep. Many of Spagna's friends and family were the subjects of his pioneering studies.

Arnold Scheibel drawing of brainstem details.

By using the Golgi method for staining neurons, it was possible for scientists like Scheibel to show that the cells of the brainstem received information from all over the body. Some projected to the eye movement centers while some projected to the cortex and still others to the spinal cord. The brain stem is thus the "command center" for the brain's neurons.

Arnold Scheibel drawing of reticular neurons.

Another Arnold Scheibel drawing.

Alvin Berman photograph of a cross section of a cat's brainstem, from his brain atlas.

Like star maps, these pages of the atlas of the cat brainstem enable scientists to aim their probes at specific cellular regions and to explore both the spontaneous and chemically induced activity of the neurons.

Alvin Berman photograph of a sagittal cross section of a cat's cerebellum and brainstem, from his brain atlas.

Alvin Berman photograph of a horizontal cross section of a cat's hypothalamus and brainstem, from his brain atlas.

Clinton Woolsey illustration of the effects on movement of the stimulation of various locations in the cortex.

The ability of neurons to control movement and to sense the environment can be studied by electrical stimulation and recording techniques. Clinton Woolsey pioneered these methods and communicated his findings via his figurine and animalculi drawings.

Another Clinton Woolsey illustration of the effects on movement of the stimulation of various locations in the cortex.

Clinton Woolsey illustration of how a rabbit's body is mapped to specific locations in its brain.

David Scott and Ruth Bleier electron-micrographic photographs of cilia in the ventricles of the brain.

The brain has hollow chambers called ventricles which are filled with crystal clear liquid. This cerebrospinal fluid (or CSF) is made by a specialized blood filter system called the choroid plexus shown above and on the left. The functions of the CSF are poorly understood but it may serve to keep the brain afloat and could allow internal communication. On the floor of these canals are delicate fronds called cilia, which float as in a tidal pool, and strange looking creatures that appear to be cleaning the surface of the ventricles.

The Dreaming Brain: How the brain creates both the sense and the nonsense of dreams

Basic Books, 1988. This is the foundational text which contrasts Hobson's new theory of dreaming with that of Freud. Paperback version is still in print and available in bookstores. "[Hobson] provides a basis for a formal analysis of dreams as behavior that is not only valuable as a contribution to the field but is critical if we are to proceed in the principal challenge of neurobiology, the understanding of the human mind.." -- from a review by Robert Y. Moore

Sleep

Scientific American Library, 1989. Covers basic sleep and dream research in all of its aspects from biological rhythms to sleep medicine. Out of print.

Dreaming as Delirium: How the Brain Goes Out of Its Mind

MIT Press 1994. Same text as The Chemistry of Conscious States. Limited availability.

Consciousness

Scientific American Library 1999. A review of the new science of consciousness as it is informed by the study of sleep and dreaming. No longer in print.

The Dream Drugstore: Chemically Altered States of Consciousness

MIT Press 2001. The pharmacological tests of the reciprocal interaction hypothesis are reviewed in the context of psychopharmacology. The book also champions first person accounts of exceptional mental states as described by Aldous Huxley (hypnosis), Albert Hoffmann (LSD), Thomas de Quincey (opium), Heinrich Kluver (mescaline), and Samuel Coleridge (morphine). Limited availability.

Dreaming: An Introduction to the Science of Sleep

Oxford University Press 2002. First published as a trade book and now available reissued in the "A Very Short Introduction" series, this user-friendly and concise text summarizes most of the new findings of sleep and dream science integrated according to the reciprocal interaction and activation synthesis models. Ideal for classroom teaching. Available from Oxford.

13 Dreams Freud Never Had: The New Mind Science

Pi Press 2005. A radical critique of Freudian dream theory which is based on neurobiology and the formal analysis of dream content. By discussing, in detail, 13 of his own dreams, the author insists that meaning and emotional salience of dreams is superficially obvious and that dreams need no deep interpretation to be psychologically informative. Limited availability.

Dream Life: An Experimental Memoir

MIT Press, 2011. A "biopsychography" discussing biology and psychology in the context of autobiographical experiences from the author's life and work. Illustrated.

Out of Its Mind: Psychiatry in Crisis - A Call for Reform

By J. Allan Hobson and Jonathan A. Leonard

Taking us first on a grand tour of the brain, the authors then draw from recent advances in cognitive therapy, brain science, animal behavior, and genetics as they explore the diagnosis and treatment of a wide range of mental ills. In the process, they show how patients with everything from mild anxiety disorders to major depression and schizophrenia would benefit from their approach.

Sleep and Dreaming: Scientific Advances and Reconsiderations

Edited by Edward F. Pace-Schott, Mark Solms, Mark Blagrove, and Stevan Harnad

This book presents five papers by leading scientists at the center of the current firmament and more than seventy-five commentaries on those papers by nearly all the other leading authorities in the field. Topics include mechanisms of dreaming in REM sleep, memory consolidation in REM sleep, and an evolutionary hypothesis of the function of dreaming.

From Angels to Neurones: Art and the New Science of Dreaming

By J. Allan Hobson and Hellmut Wohl

This book presents a new scientific theory of dreaming which is based upon the findings of brain research and traces the development of thinking about dreaming in Western Art from the classical to the modern period.

Consciousness and Cognition: An International Journal

Edited by Bernard J. Baars and William P. Banks

The studies reported in the following articles are aimed at providing a comprehensive, detailed, and quantitative picture of cognition in human dreaming.
